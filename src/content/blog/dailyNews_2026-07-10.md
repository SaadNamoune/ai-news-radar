### [LatentReasoningNoDecode: failed attempt to enable latent-space reasoning in LLMs](https://github.com/Oli-26/LatentReasoningNoDecode/blob/main/BLOG.md)

<small>**Hacker News - Newest: "llm"** &nbsp;·&nbsp; 2026-07-10 16:50:34 &nbsp;·&nbsp; `latent-reasoning` `chain-of-thought` `llm-architecture`</small>

![LatentReasoningNoDecode: failed attempt to enable latent-space reasoning in LLMs](https://opengraph.githubassets.com/00c93c30bd078b5d268528ad6ee8966a72160b36bfe7a084f3a47f661d2a5583/Oli-26/LatentReasoningNoDecode)

**Overview:** This blog post documents a failed attempt to enable latent-space reasoning in LLMs by having the model "think" in hidden states without intermediate token decoding. The author finds that standard metrics (training loss, distillation loss, benchmark improvements) can be misleading without proper ablations.

**Method:** The proposed architecture freezes a Qwen2.5-1.5B-Instruct model and, during uncertain generation steps (entropy spike), clones the hidden state into K parallel streams. Each stream "thinks" by feeding its hidden state back as input for N steps, then injects the compressed result into context. The author evaluates using logit lens diagnostics, zero/shuffle ablations, and benchmark metrics.

**Results:** Despite promising metrics (e.g., distillation loss drop from 1.98 to 0.42, latent-state entropy reduction), the ablations reveal the latent channel is ignored by the decoder. Zeroing or shuffling latent "thoughts" often matched or outperformed using them (e.g., 20% vs 30% accuracy in final eval). The author concludes that discretizing thought at every step (standard CoT) may be necessary to force commitment to the training distribution.

**Impact:** Highlights pitfalls in evaluating latent-space reasoning architectures. Demonstrates the importance of rigorous ablations (e.g., zero/shuffle controls) when claiming "reasoning" improvements. Suggests that token-level discretization may be a fundamental requirement for reliable chain-of-thought behavior.

### [Theoretical Analysis of In-Context Search: Sampling Complexity and Posterior Updates in Reasoning Traces](https://arxiv.org/abs/2607.06720)

<small>**cs.AI updates on arXiv.org** &nbsp;·&nbsp; 2026-07-10 05:00:00 &nbsp;·&nbsp; `in-context-learning` `reasoning-models` `theoretical-analysis` `sampling-complexity`</small>

**Overview:** This work provides a theoretical framework for in-context search (ICS), where LLMs iteratively generate, critique, and revise solution attempts. It models ICS as approximate inference over reasoning traces, with self-reflection acting as posterior updates. **Method:** The analysis formalizes ICS as a Bayesian inference process: the base model defines a prior, and self-reflection provides feedback to update the posterior. It derives sampling complexity bounds, showing exponential improvements in success probability when reflections localize mistakes, and polynomial complexity otherwise. The theory also shows robustness to approximate posterior updates and learnability via cross-entropy training on search rollouts. **Results:** Key predictions are validated on real large reasoning models, confirming theoretical insights about the benefits of ICS. **Impact:** Establishes foundational understanding of ICS, bridging theory and practice for reasoning models and enabling principled design of iterative reasoning systems.

### [Local Linear Transformer (LLT): Efficient Neural Operator Learning for PDEs with Linear Attention and Local Mixing](https://arxiv.org/abs/2607.07718)

<small>**cs.LG updates on arXiv.org** &nbsp;·&nbsp; 2026-07-10 05:00:00 &nbsp;·&nbsp; `neural-operators` `transformers` `PDEs` `efficient-attention`</small>

**Overview:** This paper presents the Local Linear Transformer (LLT), a novel architecture for learning PDE solution operators that addresses two key limitations of standard attention: quadratic scaling and lack of local bias. LLT combines linear global attention with local spatial mixing and integrates coordinate and geometry information, enabling efficient and accurate operator learning across diverse PDE problems. **Method:** LLT employs linear attention (e.g., Performer-style) to reduce complexity and incorporates local spatial mixing modules to capture fine-grained interactions. The architecture is evaluated on elasticity, plasticity, airfoil flow, pipe flow, and Darcy flow problems, with data spanning finite-element, finite-volume, and finite-difference discretizations on structured and unstructured meshes. **Results:** LLT achieves competitive or lower relative L2 error compared to neural-operator and transformer baselines. On matched structured discretizations, it reduces wall-clock training time per iteration by 1.8–2.5× versus Transolver. The method scales to 3D car aerodynamics with 32,186 unstructured mesh points per sample. **Impact:** Advances neural operator learning by enabling accurate, efficient, and generalizable PDE modeling across discretizations and mesh types, with potential to accelerate scientific computing and simulation workflows.

### [Controllability-Aware Black-Box Transfer Attacks on LLM-Based Network Intrusion Detection Systems](https://arxiv.org/abs/2607.07739)

<small>**cs.CR updates on arXiv.org** &nbsp;·&nbsp; 2026-07-10 05:00:00 &nbsp;·&nbsp; `adversarial-attacks` `network-intrusion-detection` `LLM-security` `transfer-learning`</small>

**Overview:** This paper investigates the adversarial robustness of LLM-based network intrusion detection systems (IDS) under realistic attacker constraints. It introduces a controllability-aware black-box transfer framework to evaluate LLM vulnerability to adversarial examples, addressing a critical gap in understanding LLM security in network security applications. **Method:** The framework partitions flow features into directly controllable (DC), indirectly controllable (IC), and uncontrollable (UC) groups based on network semantics. Perturbations are restricted to DC features while IC/UC features are frozen. Adversarial examples are generated using finite-difference PGD, greedy coordinate-wise, and NES methods via a shared XGBoost surrogate. These examples are transferred to seven LLM targets and two conventional ML targets across five IDS benchmarks (1999–2022). **Results:** Across 27 LLM configurations and 500,000+ adversarial examples, LLM transfer vulnerability is substantial but dataset-dependent. LLMs are more vulnerable than LightGBM on RT-IoT2022 and CIC-IDS-2018, comparable on NSL-KDD and UNSW-NB15, and less vulnerable on HIKARI-2021. Gradient- and score-based perturbations transfer more effectively than greedy perturbations across all models. Cross-surrogate validation with tree, neural, and linear surrogates yields similar LLM attack success rates (ASR), reducing XGBoost-specific bias. **Impact:** Advances adversarial robustness research in network security, highlights LLM vulnerabilities in IDS, and provides a reusable framework for evaluating LLM security under realistic constraints.

### [ScopeJudge: Pre-Execution Gating for LLM-Based Offensive Security Agents](https://arxiv.org/abs/2607.07774)

**Overview:** This paper addresses the challenge of enforcing engagement boundaries for LLM-based offensive security agents, where a single out-of-scope tool call can disrupt production or void bug-bounty findings. It introduces ScopeJudge, a pre-execution gating system that dynamically infers scope from user intent, addressing a critical safety gap in autonomous security operations. **Method:** ScopeJudge uses a trusted LLM judge to inspect each tool call proposed by a strong agent before execution. It introduces a benchmark of 4,897 tool calls (7.7% scope violations) labeled by professional penetration testers (Fleiss κ = 0.64, F1 = 0.78). Eight judge models are evaluated under five transcript strategies, from static policy to full raw transcript, to chart cost-accuracy trade-offs. **Results:** Static policies are structurally insufficient for scope enforcement, as judge recall collapses to near zero without request context. Two operating points are recommended: a cost-sensitive configuration and a recall-first one for high-stakes deployments. The ScopeJudge dataset is released to support real-time monitoring and scalable oversight. **Impact:** Advances safety in autonomous offensive security agents, provides a reusable dataset and framework for scope enforcement, and highlights the necessity of request-conditioned monitoring in high-stakes deployments.

**Overview:** Reforge addresses the unreliability of evaluating LLMs in binary analysis by introducing a provenance-tracked pipeline for constructing function-level ground truth from C source through compilation, DWARF extraction, and decompilation. <small>**cs.LG updates on arXiv.org** &nbsp;·&nbsp; 2026-07-10 05:00:00 &nbsp;·&nbsp; `temporal-graph-networks` `explainable-ai` `graph-neural-networks` `attribution-methods`</small>

**Overview:** This paper introduces MemExplainer, a method to explain predictions of Temporal Graph Networks (TGNs) by attributing influence through two novel structures: the topology attribution tree and memory backtracking tree. The approach addresses a critical gap in existing TGN explainability methods, which overlook the memory module's role in recording and updating node histories. **Method:** MemExplainer combines (1) a topology attribution tree to capture neighbor influence and memory vectors, and (2) a memory backtracking tree to quantify how historical events shape node memory vectors. The method applies Layer-wise Relevance Propagation (LRP) to TGNs, ensuring the sum of event contributions matches the model's logits. It further optimizes top-k selection to address nonlinearity in the logits-to-probabilities mapping. **Results:** Evaluated on nine temporal graph datasets across node property prediction, link prediction, and graph classification tasks, MemExplainer outperforms state-of-the-art baselines in providing faithful explanations. **Impact:** Advances explainable AI for dynamic graphs by integrating memory-aware attribution, opening new directions for trustworthy temporal graph learning and interpretability research.

### [ReCoLoRA: Recursive Consolidation of Low-Rank Adapters for Continual Fine-Tuning of LLMs](https://arxiv.org/abs/2607.07719)

<small>**cs.LG updates on arXiv.org** &nbsp;·&nbsp; 2026-07-10 05:00:00 &nbsp;·&nbsp; `continual-learning` `parameter-efficient-fine-tuning` `low-rank-adaptation` `large-language-models`</small>

**Overview:** ReCoLoRA introduces a spectrum-aware framework for continual fine-tuning of large language models (LLMs) using low-rank adapters (LoRA). It addresses catastrophic forgetting in sequential task adaptation by recursively consolidating adapter updates into the model's effective weight before introducing new tasks. **Method:** ReCoLoRA initializes adapters via randomized SVD of the pretrained weight, selects per-layer effective ranks using an elbow criterion, and adapts the principal subspace before opening residual capacity. Before each new task, it re-decomposes the current effective weight into a frozen residual, a slowly updated principal component, and a fresh adapter (recursive consolidation), ensuring each task starts from a model that has absorbed prior knowledge. **Results:** Evaluated on a six-task continual GLUE sequence across four 7–8B parameter backbones, ReCoLoRA achieves the best final average score on three of four backbones compared to rank-swept LoRA, PiSSA, AdaLoRA, and DoRA baselines, while training fewer parameters. An oracle-routed task-bank variant serves as an upper bound. **Impact:** Advances parameter-efficient continual learning for LLMs by mitigating forgetting and improving knowledge retention across task sequences, with implications for scalable and sustainable model adaptation in dynamic environments.

[→ Read full article](https://arxiv.org/abs/2607.07719)

### [CTA-pipelining: Latency-Oriented Spatial Scaling for Multi-GPU Systems](https://arxiv.org/abs/2607.07862)
